Clermont's hills make algae directional

Most of the metro is flat; Clermont is not, and the elevation changes how roofs weather. Strong, open sun on south- and west-facing slopes keeps them dry and largely streak-free, while north-facing slopes hold morning dew longest and grow algae almost alone. The result is the classic Clermont complaint: a roof that looks fine from the driveway and streaked from the backyard. Cleaning still treats the whole roof — colonies seed across slopes — but the regrowth pattern here genuinely is directional.

The housing mix is mostly newer shingle construction in the master-planned corridors off US-27 and Hartwood Marsh, with tile appearing in the newer communities, plus established neighborhoods near downtown and the chain of lakes with more shade and more moss. HOA enforcement is active in the newer communities, so notice-driven scheduling is as routine here as anywhere in the metro.
