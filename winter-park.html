Canopy is the whole story in Winter Park

The oaks over the brick streets keep Winter Park roofs shaded and damp, which is the exact recipe roof algae, moss, and lichen want. Slopes under canopy north of Fairbanks and around the chain of lakes stay wet hours longer after each storm, and oak debris in the valleys feeds growth between cleanings. The practical result: Winter Park roofs run a shorter cleaning cycle than open-lot suburbs, and moss and lichen — the growth types that actually shorten roof life — show up here more than algae-only markets.

The housing stock adds its own wrinkle. Many Winter Park homes carry older roofs, architectural shingle, and tile, often on steep historic rooflines with tight side yards and mature landscaping under every drip edge. That is precisely the situation where method matters: low-pressure soft washing protects older shingles that have no granules to spare, and careful plant protection matters when the beds under the roofline took twenty years to grow.

Frequently asked questions

Why does my Winter Park roof streak faster than my last house?

Almost certainly canopy. Shade keeps slopes damp longer after rain and dew, and damp time is what drives algae, moss, and lichen growth. Tree-covered Winter Park lots simply run a shorter cleaning cycle.

My roof is older — is cleaning still safe?

With soft washing, yes — low pressure is exactly what an older shingle surface needs. What an older roof cannot tolerate is pressure washing, and if the roof is near replacement, you should be told before anyone takes your money.

Do you handle the lakefront streets?

Yes — lake-adjacent lots are a regular part of the area, with runoff handling planned accordingly.
