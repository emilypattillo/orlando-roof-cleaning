Oviedo's two roof populations

Oviedo splits into two roof stories. The established neighborhoods — 80s and 90s subdivisions under now-mature oaks — behave like classic Seminole County canopy territory: shaded shingle slopes, fast algae regrowth, moss in the valleys, and active HOAs that notice. The newer construction east toward the 419 corridor runs the opposite pattern: open lots, full sun, and roofs that stay cleaner longer but show their first streaks dramatically because the shingles started spotless.

Both populations share the Central Florida constant: humid mornings and afternoon storms that keep spores moving street to street. In the established areas especially, one streaky roof becomes a block of them within a few seasons — which is also why Oviedo HOAs tend to send roof letters in batches. If your neighbors are getting notices, yours is a season behind.

Frequently asked questions

My Oviedo subdivision is sending out HOA roof letters. Coincidence?

No — algae spreads by airborne spores, so streets streak together, and associations often sweep a whole neighborhood in one inspection cycle. Getting ahead of the letter is cheaper than answering it.

My house is newer construction. Why is it streaking already?

Spores do not check the build date. New shingles in full sun resist longer, but once colonies establish they spread on the same schedule — and streaks show more on a roof that started spotless.
